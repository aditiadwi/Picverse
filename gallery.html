Guernica
Pablo Picasso
Year: 1937
Medium: Oil on canvas
Dimensions: 349.3 × 776.6 cm
Location: Museo Reina Sofía, Madrid

Description

Guernica is one of Picasso's most famous works, created in response to the bombing of Guernica, a Basque town in northern Spain, by Nazi Germany and Fascist Italy at the request of the Spanish Nationalists during the Spanish Civil War. The painting, which uses a palette of gray, black, and white, is regarded as one of the most powerful anti-war paintings in history.

The market gardens of Vaugirard
Paul Gauguin
Year: 1879
Medium: Oil on canvas
Dimensions: 64.80 × 100 cm
Location: Smith College Museum of Art (SCMA), Northampton, MA, US

Description

"The market gardens of Vaugirard" captures a serene view of the Parisian suburbs in 1879, during Gauguin's early artistic career. The painting depicts the productive gardens of Vaugirard, which was then on the outskirts of Paris (now incorporated into the city).

In the foreground, Gauguin presents a pathway and lush trees that guide the viewer's eye into the composition. His characteristic Impressionist brushstrokes create a sense of life and movement in the foliage without becoming entangled in excessive detail.

The middle ground showcases carefully cultivated vegetable garden plots, indicating the productive agricultural activity typical of the area. Houses and buildings are rendered in relatively subdued tones, with red-tinted rooftops providing a warm contrast against the predominantly green and blue landscape.

Gauguin's skill in capturing atmosphere is evident in the sky, painted with various gradations of blue and white, reflecting weather conditions and creating a sense of openness that characterizes the Impressionist approach to depicting natural light and its effect on landscape.

Overall, the painting radiates the tranquility of daily life in a developing suburb, offering viewers a glimpse of French rural life in the late 19th century, before Gauguin developed his better-known Post-Impressionist style.

The Vision after the Sermon (Jacob Wrestling with the Angel)
Paul Gauguin
Year: 1888
Medium: Oil on canvas
Dimensions: 72.2 × 91 cm
Location: Scottish National Gallery, Edinburgh

Description

"The Vision after the Sermon (Jacob Wrestling with the Angel)" is one of Paul Gauguin's most significant works, marking his departure from Impressionism toward a more symbolic style that would define his later career. Created during his time in Brittany, this painting depicts Breton women who, after hearing a sermon about Jacob's biblical struggle with an angel, experience a collective vision of this sacred event.

The bold composition divides the canvas with a diagonal tree trunk, separating the observing women in their traditional white headdresses and black dresses from the mystical vision occurring in a field of vivid red - a color chosen not for realism but for emotional impact. The flattened perspective, simplified forms, and arbitrary color use demonstrate Gauguin's revolutionary approach that would influence modern art movements.

This painting exemplifies Gauguin's "Synthetism," combining observed reality, memory, and emotion to create a symbolic representation rather than a literal scene. The work's daring color, spiritual theme, and non-naturalistic approach signaled Gauguin's pioneering role in Post-Impressionism and his growing interest in exploring primitive cultures and religious symbolism that would later lead him to Tahiti.

Van Gogh Painting Sunflowers
Paul Gauguin
Year: 1888
Medium: Oil on canvas
Dimensions: 73 × 91 cm
Location: Van Gogh Museum, Amsterdam

Description

"Van Gogh Painting Sunflowers" is a remarkable portrait created during Gauguin's brief but intense period of cohabitation with Vincent van Gogh in Arles in late 1888. This work depicts Gauguin's friend and fellow artist engaged in painting his famous sunflower series, capturing a pivotal moment in art history when these two influential Post-Impressionists lived and worked together in the "Yellow House."

In this painting, Gauguin employs his distinctive Synthetist style with simplified forms, bold outlines, and non-naturalistic colors. Rather than creating a literal representation of the scene, Gauguin presents a symbolic interpretation of Van Gogh at work. The composition shows Van Gogh in profile, deeply concentrated on his canvas, with his characteristic red beard and intense focus that Gauguin observed firsthand.

This work holds particular historical significance as it was created just weeks before the infamous incident where Van Gogh cut off part of his ear following a heated argument with Gauguin, effectively ending their collaboration. The painting thus serves as a document of their complex friendship and artistic exchange that, though brief, had a profound impact on both artists' development and on the course of modern art.

Banks of the Oise
Paul Gauguin
Year: 1881
Medium: Oil on canvas
Dimensions: 34 × 54 cm
Location: Private Collection

Description

"Banks of the Oise" represents an important transitional period in Gauguin's artistic development, painted during his early years when he was still heavily influenced by the Impressionist movement. Created five years before he would abandon his career as a stockbroker to pursue art full-time, this landscape depicts the tranquil Oise River in northern France, a popular subject for Impressionist painters including Pissarro, who mentored Gauguin.

The painting demonstrates Gauguin's growing mastery of Impressionist techniques, with its emphasis on natural light, atmospheric effects, and visible brushwork that captures the reflections on the water's surface and the verdant vegetation along the riverbank. The composition balances the horizontal expanse of the river with vertical elements of trees, creating a serene vision of the French countryside.

While this work still shows Gauguin operating within the Impressionist style he would later reject, it contains subtle hints of his developing interest in color harmonies and simplified forms that would eventually evolve into his distinctive Post-Impressionist approach. This painting thus serves as a fascinating document of Gauguin's artistic journey before his radical departure to more symbolic and primitive styles that would define his mature work.

Port de Grenelle
Paul Gauguin
Year: 1875
Medium: Oil on canvas
Dimensions: 32 × 41 cm
Location: Private Collection

Description

"Port de Grenelle" is one of Gauguin's earliest known paintings, created when he was still an amateur artist and working full-time as a successful stockbroker. This urban landscape depicts the industrial area of Grenelle along the Seine River in Paris, featuring factories and working vessels that characterized this port district in the late 19th century.

This work reflects Gauguin's early style when he was heavily influenced by the Barbizon School and the emerging Impressionist movement. The composition demonstrates his initial approach to capturing light and atmosphere, with subtle color variations and attention to the reflections on the water's surface. The muted palette and careful attention to realistic detail stand in stark contrast to the bold colors and simplified forms that would define his later Post-Impressionist works.

Historically significant as one of Gauguin's formative works, "Port de Grenelle" offers a fascinating glimpse into the artist's beginnings before his radical stylistic evolution. Painted six years before he would abandon his financial career to pursue art full-time, this early landscape reveals Gauguin's developing eye and technical skill even as he was still finding his distinctive artistic voice.

Full Moon Service
Erik Johansson
Year: 2017
Medium: Digital photo manipulation
Dimensions: 68 × 51 cm (26.8 × 20.1 inches)
Location: Artist's limited edition collection

Description

This is a photo I've been working on from now and then for the past 8 months. Shot in late summer of 2016 a beautiful calm evening in the middle of Sweden. I brought out 7 rice lamps, 7 light bulbs, an electric generator, a car and two models out in a field. The main part that has been retouched in this photo is to replace the rice ball with a moon texture, the light and the mood was very close to what you see in the photo.

Erik Johansson is renowned for his surreal photography that combines multiple exposures and digital manipulation to create impossible scenes that challenge our perception of reality. His meticulous planning and execution process often involves months of preparation, as seen in this work where he transported elaborate lighting equipment to a remote Swedish field to achieve the perfect atmospheric conditions.

The seamless integration of practical lighting effects with digital post-production demonstrates Johansson's mastery of both traditional photography techniques and modern digital artistry. The work exemplifies his signature style of creating dreamlike scenarios that appear fantastical yet maintain a photographic authenticity that makes viewers question what is real and what is manipulated.

Regression
Kauê Daiprai
Year: 2020
Medium: Digital art / 3D rendering
Dimensions: Variable digital format
Location: Artist's digital portfolio

Description

"Regression" by Brazilian digital artist Kauê Daiprai is a striking example of contemporary 3D digital art that explores themes of transformation and materiality. The artwork features biomorphic forms that appear to be simultaneously solid and liquid, frozen in a moment of metamorphosis between states of matter.

Daiprai is known for his experimental approach to digital sculpture, creating works that challenge our perception of physical properties through hyperrealistic textures and innovative rendering techniques. In "Regression," the artist uses a sophisticated color palette of metallic silvers and reflective surfaces to create forms that seem to exist in a liminal space between the organic and inorganic worlds.

The piece demonstrates Daiprai's technical mastery of 3D modeling software and his artistic vision that pushes the boundaries of digital materiality. His work is part of a growing movement in digital art that explores posthuman aesthetics and speculative futures, creating virtual objects that could not exist in physical reality but provoke profound questions about our relationship with technology and the natural world.

Pemburuan Banteng (Bull Hunting)
Raden Saleh
Year: 1855
Medium: Oil on canvas
Dimensions: 110 × 180 cm
Location: National Gallery of Indonesia, Jakarta

Description

"Pemburuan Banteng" (Bull Hunting) is one of Raden Saleh's most celebrated masterpieces, exemplifying his unique blend of European Romantic style with Indonesian themes. Created during the period when the artist had returned to Java after years of studying and working in Europe, this dramatic hunting scene captures a moment of intense action as hunters on horseback pursue wild bulls through a tumultuous landscape.

The painting showcases Saleh's exceptional technical skills with its dynamic composition, dramatic lighting, and meticulous attention to anatomical detail in both human and animal figures. The swirling movement of horses, hunters, and bulls creates a palpable sense of energy and danger. This work is characteristic of Saleh's fascination with depicting the power of nature and the struggle between humans and animals, themes that appeared frequently in his oeuvre.

Raden Saleh, often considered Indonesia's first modern painter, was remarkable for his ability to combine the European Romantic tradition he absorbed during his years in the Netherlands, Germany, and France with subjects and settings from his homeland. "Pemburuan Banteng" represents not only a significant artistic achievement but also an important cultural document that bridges Eastern and Western artistic traditions during the colonial period in Indonesia.

Bali Life
Lee Man Fong
Year: 1960s
Medium: Oil on canvas
Dimensions: 88 × 122 cm
Location: Private Collection

Description

"Bali Life" exemplifies Lee Man Fong's masterful fusion of Eastern and Western artistic traditions, capturing the serene daily activities of Balinese villagers. This painting demonstrates the artist's signature elongated horizontal format, influenced by traditional Chinese scroll paintings, while depicting Indonesian subject matter with techniques acquired during his European training.

The composition portrays Balinese women engaged in everyday rituals and work, their graceful forms rendered with meticulous attention to detail and harmonious arrangement across the canvas. Lee's distinctive soft palette and delicate brushwork create an atmosphere of tranquility and timelessness, idealizing the rural Balinese lifestyle that fascinated many artists during this period.

Lee Man Fong, born in China and later becoming an Indonesian citizen, was one of Southeast Asia's most significant 20th-century artists. After studying in the Netherlands and becoming court painter to President Sukarno, he developed a unique artistic identity that bridged multiple cultural traditions. His Balinese paintings like this one are particularly valued for their cultural sensitivity and technical excellence, representing an important chapter in the development of modern Indonesian art.

Borobudur and the Sun
Affandi
Year: 1963
Medium: Oil on canvas
Dimensions: 95 × 145 cm
Location: Affandi Museum, Yogyakarta, Indonesia

Description

"Borobudur and the Sun" is a powerful example of Affandi's expressive style that revolutionized Indonesian modern art. In this dynamic composition, the ancient Buddhist temple of Borobudur is depicted beneath a blazing sun, with the artist's characteristic swirling, energetic brushstrokes conveying both the physical structure and its spiritual significance in the Indonesian cultural landscape.

Affandi's unique technique of applying paint directly from the tube and manipulating it with his fingers, hands, and occasionally the brush handle is evident in the thick impasto and gestural marks that define this work. The vibrant colors and emotional intensity reflect his philosophy that painting should capture not just the visual appearance but the emotional experience of seeing. The sun, a recurring motif in Affandi's oeuvre, radiates with an almost supernatural energy above the temple, creating a dialogue between natural and human-made monuments.

Considered one of Indonesia's most important artists, Affandi developed his expressionistic style independently of Western influences, though his work has been compared to figures like Vincent van Gogh and Chaim Soutine. This painting of Borobudur, a national symbol of Indonesia, demonstrates his deep connection to his cultural heritage while expressing it through his distinctive visual language that prioritized emotional truth over realistic representation.

Women Around the Lotus Pond
Adrien-Jean Le Mayeur de Merprès
Year: 1950-1951
Medium: Oil on canvas
Dimensions: 100 × 120 cm
Location: Le Mayeur Museum, Sanur, Bali

Description

"Women Around the Lotus Pond" exemplifies Adrien-Jean Le Mayeur's captivating portrayals of Balinese life, particularly focusing on the grace and beauty of Balinese women in a lush tropical setting. The Belgian-born artist settled in Bali in 1932 and was enchanted by the island's culture, eventually marrying his favorite model, the Legong dancer Ni Pollok, who appears frequently in his works.

This painting showcases Le Mayeur's signature Impressionist style adapted to tropical light and color, with vibrant hues capturing the sun-dappled garden of his beachfront home in Sanur. The composition features graceful Balinese women in traditional dress gathered around a lotus pond, engaged in typical daily activities or ceremonial preparations. The interplay of light filtering through tropical foliage creates a dreamlike atmosphere that characterizes Le Mayeur's idealized vision of Bali as a paradise untouched by modernization.

Le Mayeur's work holds significant historical value as documentation of pre-tourism Bali, though viewed through a distinctly European aesthetic lens. His paintings gained international recognition for their beauty and exoticism, and are now considered important examples of the foreign artistic perspective on Balinese culture during the colonial period. The Le Mayeur Museum in Sanur, formerly the artist's home and studio, preserves many of his works in their original setting.

Pandawa Dadu
Hendra Gunawan
Year: 1971
Medium: Oil on canvas
Dimensions: 95 × 140 cm
Location: Ciputra Collection, Indonesia

Description

"Pandawa Dadu" (The Pandavas Gambling) depicts an iconic scene from the Mahabharata epic where the five Pandava brothers lose their kingdom in a rigged dice game. This masterpiece exemplifies Hendra Gunawan's distinctive style that combines elements of traditional Indonesian wayang (shadow puppet) aesthetics with bold, expressive modernism.

Created during Gunawan's imprisonment for his alleged connections to communist organizations, this painting reflects his deep connection to Indonesian cultural narratives even in times of personal hardship. The elongated figures with exaggerated proportions and vibrant colors are hallmarks of Gunawan's unique artistic language, which evolved from his early training in revolutionary art collectives during Indonesia's struggle for independence.

The dramatic scene captures the tension of the fateful gambling match, with the artist's characteristic distortion of form and expressive use of color conveying the emotional and moral complexities of the story. This work demonstrates why Hendra Gunawan is considered one of Indonesia's most important modern artists—his ability to merge traditional narratives with innovative techniques created a visual language that was both distinctly Indonesian and boldly contemporary. His works often address themes of social justice, cultural identity, and moral dilemmas, making them both aesthetically striking and philosophically profound.
